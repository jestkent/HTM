Measuring Distance

Stellar Parallax

Imagine holding your finger out and closing one eye, then the other. Your finger seems to jump back and forth against the background. This is parallax.

Astronomers use this same trick! As Earth orbits the Sun, we look at a nearby star from different positions. The star appears to shift slightly against the background of more distant stars. By measuring this angle, we can calculate the exact distance.

Earth's position changes by 186 million miles every 6 months!

Standard Candles

For stars too far away for parallax, we use "Standard Candles" like Cepheid Variables. These are special stars that pulse with brightness. The speed of their pulse tells us exactly how bright they truly are. Measuring Size

The Stefan-Boltzmann Law

We can't usually measure a star's size directly because they are too far away and just look like dots. Instead, we use physics!

If we know a star's Temperature (from its color) and its Luminosity (total energy output), we can calculate its surface area. From there, we get the radius.
